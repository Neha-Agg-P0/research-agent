# FA Research Agent — Domain Context

## What This Tool Is

An external intelligence tool for strategy, growth, product, and advisor-facing program leaders at firms that serve financial advisors. Think LPL Financial, Commonwealth, Raymond James, Osaic — their internal teams who need sharp external signals to structure thinking on new programs, products, and strategies for the advisors they serve.

This is NOT a tool for advisors themselves. The user is an internal leader asking: "What should we build next, and what does the market tell us?"

---

## The Six Strategic Lenses

Every query should be analyzed through whichever of these lenses are relevant. Always note when findings vary by advisor segment.

### 1. Data & Insights
What data products, analytics, and research consulting do advisors need?
- White space: what are advisors asking for that no one offers yet?
- Competitive: what data/insights products do other BDs, custodians, and TAMPs already offer?
- Examples: practice benchmarking data, client segmentation analytics, portfolio analytics, peer comparison tools

### 2. Growth & Benchmarks
What growth programs, benchmark studies, and practice management resources exist?
- Key benchmark studies: Cerulli Associates, Schwab RIA Benchmarking Study, Fidelity RIA Benchmarking, InvestmentNews Adviser Research, FA Magazine benchmarks
- Program types: coaching programs, study groups, growth accelerators, FPA and XYPN resources, custodian-sponsored programs
- Flag when a competitor launches or updates a growth program

### 3. Technology & AI
What tools, platforms, and AI use cases are being built for or adopted by advisors?
- Categories (Kitces Technology Map): CRM, financial planning software, portfolio management, compliance, client portal, document automation, AI meeting notes, prospecting tools
- Notable companies: Zocks AI, Jump AI, Finny, Orion, Riskalyze/Nitrogen, MoneyGuidePro, eMoney, Wealthbox, Redtail, Salesforce Financial Services Cloud
- Signal: adoption rates, new integrations, BD/custodian technology partnerships, advisor AI use case adoption

### 4. Operations & Efficiency
What back-office and operational solutions are advisors adopting?
- Back-office: trading automation, rebalancing, billing, performance reporting, compliance monitoring
- Front-office efficiency: client onboarding, document management, workflow automation
- Distinguish between what solos need vs what enterprise firms need — very different problems

### 5. HNW Capabilities
What capabilities are being built for advisors serving high-net-worth and ultra-HNW clients?
- Topics: alternative investments, tax planning, estate planning, trust services, lending, insurance, family office services
- Who's building: custodians adding alternatives access, TAMPs adding HNW tiers, BDs expanding managed account platforms
- Segment signal: HNW capabilities matter most for ensemble and enterprise advisors; less relevant for solo practices

### 6. Investor Strategy
What are end-clients and investors demanding from their advisors?
- Topics: retirement income planning, ESG/sustainable investing, financial wellness, digital advice, generational wealth transfer
- Demand signals: investor surveys, consumer research, advisor feedback on what clients are asking for
- Implication: what programs should BDs build to help advisors meet this demand?

## Advisor Segment Framework

Every finding should be tagged to the segment(s) it applies to. The same question has different answers across segments.

| Segment | Description | Typical AUM | Key needs |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Solo** | Single advisor, minimal staff | <$50M | Efficiency tools, turnkey solutions, time savings |
| **Lead Advisor** | Primary advisor + 1-2 support | $50-250M | Growth programs, client acquisition, team building |
| **Ensemble** | Multiple equal-partner advisors | $250M-$2B | Practice management, succession, shared infrastructure |
| **Enterprise** | Large multi-advisor firm | $2B+ | Technology platforms, M&A, talent, institutional solutions |
| **RIA** | Independent registered investment advisor | All sizes | Custodian services, compliance, independence tools |

## Relevance Filter

### Always relevant — keep
Content must mention at least one of: financial advisor, RIA, broker-dealer, wirehouse, wealth management, registered investment advisor, advisory firm, independent advisor, fee-only advisor, CFP, AUM, fiduciary, custodian, TAMP, practice management, advisor technology, wealthtech, fintech for advisors.

### Flag as low-confidence — label clearly
- Generic business/management content (MIT Sloan, HBR strategy articles) unless directly applied to FA context
- Consumer personal finance content (how to choose a financial advisor)
- Banking/insurance content without advisor relevance

### Discard signals
- General stock market commentary
- Macroeconomic analysis not connected to advisor business impact
- Consumer investing articles

## Key Industry Terminology

- **BD** — broker-dealer
- **RIA** — registered investment advisor
- **OSJ** — office of supervisory jurisdiction (BD branch structure)
- **TAMP** — turnkey asset management program
- **AUM** — assets under management
- **GDC/GPC** — gross dealer concession / gross production credits (advisor revenue metric)
- **Payout** — percentage of revenue advisor keeps vs pays to BD
- **Breakaway** — advisor leaving a wirehouse to go independent
- **Custodian** — firm holding client assets (Schwab, Fidelity, Pershing)
- **Rep-as-PM** — advisor managing portfolios directly rather than using models
- **Managed accounts** — discretionary portfolio management programs
- **Ensemble practice** — multi-advisor firm where partners share ownership and clients
- **Succession** — transition of advisor book of business to next generation
- **Basis points (bps)** — fee measurement; 100 bps = 1%
- **HNW/UHNW** — high-net-worth / ultra-high-net-worth (typically $1M+ / $30M+)
- **Alternatives** — non-traditional investments: private equity, hedge funds, real assets
- **Ticket charges** — per-transaction fees charged by BDs/custodians

## Output Guidance

Results should be structured as a one-pager brief suitable for a strategy meeting:
- Lead with direct answers, not summaries of sources
- Call out competitor moves by firm name
- Tag findings to advisor segments where relevant (solo / lead / ensemble / enterprise / RIA)
- Surface benchmark data (Cerulli, Schwab, Fidelity studies) prominently — these are authoritative
- Identify white space explicitly: "No major BD currently offers X" or "Advisors in Y segment report needing Z but solutions are limited"
- Every claim links to a source
